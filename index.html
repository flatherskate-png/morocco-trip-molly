01
Day 01 · Arrival
Monday, June 29

Casablanca & Jewish Heritage

A city more complex than its legend — and a Jewish heritage hiding in plain sight.

Arrival into Casablanca, check-in and a freshen-up, then lunch at one of the historic Jewish clubs. The afternoon brings the Museum of Moroccan Jewry, the Beth-El Synagogue, and a rare meeting with the leadership of the Jewish community and the founders of Association Mimouna — including El Mehdi Boudra, the remarkable architect of Muslim-Jewish co-existence in Morocco.

Morocco: largest Jewish museum in the Arab world Association Mimouna: founded 2007 Beth-El: one of Casablanca's oldest synagogues
Morning mosque visit · then road to Rabat
If you have time…
🕌
Habous Quarter, Casablanca
The 'new medina' built by the French in 1930s Moroccan style — a strange and beautiful colonial simulacrum. Good pastry shops and the Royal Palace of Casablanca nearby.
🌊
Corniche, Casablanca
The Atlantic seafront promenade stretching west from the Hassan II Mosque. Restaurants, cafés, and the city's best sunset views.
02
Day 02 · Coast & Capital
Tuesday, June 30

Hassan II Mosque & Rabat

The world's third largest mosque — built over the Atlantic — and a capital city that rewards the unhurried.

A morning visit to the extraordinary Hassan II Mosque, its minaret the tallest religious structure in the world, rising from a platform built over the sea. Then north to Rabat: the mausoleum of Kings Mohammed V and Hassan II, the Royal Palace, and the charming Mellah. Lunch along the waterfront before a late-night flight south to Errashadiya.

Hassan II Mosque: minaret 210m — world's tallest Capacity: 105,000 worshippers Rabat: UNESCO World Heritage City
Late flight south · Errashadiya · gateway to the Sahara
If you have time…
🏰
Chellah Necropolis, Rabat
A ruined Roman city and medieval Islamic necropolis inside the same walled complex — storks nesting on the minarets, Roman columns half-buried in undergrowth. Haunting and overlooked.
🎨
Oudayas Kasbah, Rabat
A blue-and-white painted hilltop fortress at the mouth of the Bou Regreg river. The garden inside is one of the most peaceful spots in Morocco.
03
Day 03 · The Sahara
Wednesday, July 1

Gateway to the Sahara

The road south ends at the dunes. The silence begins.

A visit to the historic Synagogue of Errashadiya, then travel southward through Erfoud and Merzouga to Auberge Timbouktu — your base in the Sahara. An afternoon of Gnawa music and a meeting with the Moroccan Bedouin that most travelers never encounter. The desert here is not empty; it is full.

Erg Chebbi dunes: up to 150m / 500ft high Gnawa: UNESCO Intangible Heritage 2019 Auberge Timbouktu: desert base camp
Before dawn · sunrise over 50-story dunes · then north to the Atlas
If you have time…
🐪
Camel Trek into Erg Chebbi
An hour by camel into the dunes at sunset, spending the night in a desert camp. Cold in winter, warm in July — and worth it entirely. The silence before dawn is the sound you'll remember.
🎵
Gnawa Night Music
Gnawa is a trance-music tradition brought to Morocco by sub-Saharan slaves — ceremonial, hypnotic, and recognized by UNESCO. Merzouga has some of the most authentic practitioners in the country.
04
Day 04 · High Atlas
Thursday, July 2

Sunrise on the Dunes & High Atlas

The Sahara at dawn, then the mountains by evening — two worlds in a single day.

Rise before the sun to watch it climb over dunes fifty stories tall. Then an early departure north and west to Telouet, deep in the High Atlas Mountains. Evening in the village with Agwach dancing and singing — a living Berber tradition of the highlands that has survived centuries unchanged.

Telouet Kasbah: seat of the El Glaoui dynasty High Atlas: peaks above 4,000m Agwach: traditional Berber folk performance
Departure for Marrakech · Shabbat approaches
If you have time…
🏔️
Todra Gorge
A 300-metre slot canyon of rose-red limestone, narrow enough in places to touch both walls. On the route between Merzouga and the Atlas — a stop that earns its detour.
🎬
Aït Benhaddou
A UNESCO-listed ksar (fortified village) near Ouarzazate that has appeared in Lawrence of Arabia, Gladiator, and Game of Thrones. The real thing is more impressive than any film set.
05
Day 05 · Marrakech
Friday, July 3

Marrakech & the Mellah

The ancient Jewish Quarter as Shabbat descends over the Red City.

Arrival in Marrakech and an afternoon in the Mellah — the historic Jewish Quarter, one of the oldest in Morocco. As sundown approaches, Shabbat services at the 500-year-old Al-Azama Synagogue, followed by a homemade Moroccan Shabbat dinner in the synagogue's ancient courtyard.

Al-Azama Synagogue: over 500 years old Mellah: established 1557 by the Saadian dynasty Shabbat dinner in the synagogue courtyard
Shabbat · Saturday July 4
If you have time…
🌿
Bahia Palace
A 19th-century vizier's palace of 160 rooms built to be the greatest of its time. The tiled courtyards and painted cedar ceilings are among the finest examples of Moroccan domestic architecture.
⚰️
Saadian Tombs
Sealed by a jealous sultan in the 17th century and only rediscovered in 1917 — the burial site of the Saadian dynasty, with some of the most intricate stucco and tilework in Marrakech.
06
Day 06 · Shabbat
Saturday, July 4

Shabbat in Marrakech

A day of rest — Moroccan style. Kosher lunch, cobalt gardens, and the world's most famous square after dark.

Morning services for those who wish. A traditional home-cooked kosher Shabbat lunch, then the afternoon at leisure: the extraordinary cobalt world of the Jardin Majorelle, walking tours through the city's layered neighbourhoods. As evening falls, Jemaa el-Fna — the world-famous square — comes alive, closing with a cooking lesson in Moroccan specialties.

Jardin Majorelle: designed 1923, restored by Yves Saint Laurent Jemaa el-Fna: UNESCO Intangible Heritage Home-cooked kosher Shabbat lunch
Sunday morning · south to the Souss Valley
If you have time…
🛍️
Marrakech Souks
The medina souks are organized by trade — dyers, leather workers, spice merchants, lamp makers — each quarter its own sensory world. Bargaining is expected; treat it as conversation, not combat.
🍽️
Nomad Restaurant
The best rooftop view of the Marrakech medina with modern Moroccan food. The lamb pastilla is the dish to order. Book ahead.
07
Day 07 · Taroudant
Sunday, July 5

Taroudant & Arazane

The grandmother of Marrakech — older, quieter, and less discovered.

A morning visit to the Medinah of Marrakech before departing south for Taroudant — the ancient Berber walled city in the Souss Valley, often described as a smaller, more authentic Marrakech. From there, the village of Arazane and a meeting with Harim, a keeper of remarkable local knowledge and living tradition.

Taroudant: ancient Saadian capital, 16th century Souss Valley: Argan oil heartland City walls: 7.5km intact circuit
West to the Atlantic · Essaouira
If you have time…
🫒
Argan Oil Cooperative
The Souss Valley is the only place in the world where argan trees grow wild. Women's cooperatives produce the oil by hand — a slow, labour-intensive process. Visiting one is a purchase worth making.
🏇
Taroudant Market
The Tuesday and Thursday souks in Taroudant are among the most authentic in southern Morocco — serving local Berber traders rather than tourists. Leather, spices, and live animals.

Days 08–10 · Essaouira
Monday–Wednesday, July 6–8

Essaouira

A windswept Atlantic fortress where the medina is blue and white, the fish is fresh, and the light is unlike anywhere else in Morocco.

Three days in one of Morocco's most magnificent cities. Monday brings the first intoxicating taste of this fortified port. Tuesday is full: a meeting with Moroccan high school teachers, deep exploration of the medina, the ancient ramparts, and the bustling fishing harbor. Wednesday morning in Essaouira before the road turns back toward Marrakech.

Essaouira Medina: UNESCO World Heritage since 2001 Known as "the Wind City of Africa" Gnawa Music Festival held here annually
July 9 · Morning flight Marrakech → Casablanca → New York
If you have time…
🐟
Fishing Harbor at Dawn
The blue-painted fishing boats return before sunrise. The harbor market that follows — chaotic, aromatic, utterly alive — is over by 8am. Worth the early alarm.
🎸
Gnawa Music of Essaouira
Essaouira is the spiritual home of Gnawa music — the annual festival draws 500,000 people. Year-round, the musicians play in the squares of the medina. Jimi Hendrix visited in 1969 and never quite left.

Before you go.

Packing for July

July is hot — Marrakech regularly hits 100°F+. Lightweight, loose, modest clothing is both practical and respectful. Layers for the Sahara night (cold even in summer). Good walking shoes for medina stones. Sunscreen and a hat are non-negotiable.

Shabbat Observance

Friday evening and Saturday are built into the itinerary with the Jewish communities of Marrakech. Services at Al-Azama Synagogue are offered to all, as is the kosher Shabbat lunch. No planning required — it is all arranged.

Money in Morocco

The Moroccan dirham cannot be exported, so exchange on arrival. Cash is essential in medinas, souks, and desert camps. Cards work in larger Marrakech hotels but not reliably elsewhere. Bargaining is expected in the souks — remain cheerful, it is theatre as much as commerce.

The Sahara Night

The overnight at Auberge Timbouktu is the experiential peak of the trip. Set an alarm for well before sunrise — the light on the dunes in the first fifteen minutes of day is the image you will carry home.

Photography

Always ask before photographing people in the medinas — it is both respectful and often leads to a more interesting encounter. The Hassan II Mosque interior is one of the most photographed in Africa; guided tours include it.

This Trip's Organizer

This journey is organized through Kivunim, whose Morocco programs focus on Jewish heritage, Muslim-Jewish dialogue, and deep cultural encounter. The access to communities and individuals on this itinerary is the product of years of relationships.
